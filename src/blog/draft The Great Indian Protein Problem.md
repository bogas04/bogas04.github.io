India is a very diverse country with a plethora of languages, cuisines, ingredients and diet preferences. It makes it a perfect foodie country, in my biased opinion. It also makes nutrition hard to discuss in broad strokes.

During the pandemic, my partner and I started paying closer attention to our diets. We tracked calories and protein, and that bookkeeping even motivated me to work on a small calorie-tracking app for ourselves—one that accounted for Indian ingredients with a simpler UI. Along the way, we kept running into the same question: how much protein do we actually need, and how do we get it from familiar Indian food?

This is a draft compiled from the posts I started on that question. It describes my personal approach, not medical advice.

## How much protein do we need?

Nutrition guidance can feel contradictory. Some recommendations use age and gender; others use body weight, total calories, or activity level. For example, the Dietary Reference Intake is often summarised as `0.8g` of protein per kilogram of body weight, while the acceptable macronutrient distribution range places protein at 10–35% of daily calories. Those numbers answer slightly different questions, so they do not always produce the same target.

Rather than treating a single gram number as universal, I find it more useful to estimate total energy needs first and then choose a protein ratio that fits within a balanced diet.

## Step 1: Estimate daily calories

One common starting point is a basal metabolic rate (BMR) estimate plus an activity multiplier. The Mifflin–St Jeor formula is usually written as:

| Gender | Formula |
| :----- | :------ |
| Men | `(10 × weight in kg) + (6.25 × height in cm) − (5 × age in years) + 5` |
| Women | `(10 × weight in kg) + (6.25 × height in cm) − (5 × age in years) − 161` |

The BMR is an estimate of the energy needed at rest. To estimate maintenance calories, multiply it by an activity factor:

| Activity level | Ratio |
| :------------- | ----: |
| Little to no exercise | 1.2 |
| Light exercise (1–3 days per week) | 1.375 |
| Moderate exercise (3–5 days per week) | 1.55 |
| Heavy exercise (6–7 days per week) | 1.725 |
| Very heavy exercise | 1.9 |

For example, my estimated BMR was `1656 kCal`. With a sedentary multiplier, that came to roughly `1987 kCal` per day to maintain my weight. The point is not false precision; it is to use a target that accounts for body size, age, activity and goals.

## Step 2: Pick a sensible macro range

The acceptable macronutrient distribution ranges (AMDR) are commonly given as 45–65% of daily calories from carbohydrates, 20–35% from fats, and 10–35% from protein. [Healthline has a useful overview](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/best-macronutrient-ratio).

Fat provides roughly 9 kCal per gram, while protein and carbohydrates provide roughly 4 kCal per gram. With a `1987 kCal` daily target, the ranges look like this:

| Macro | Minimum grams | Maximum grams |
| :---- | ------------: | ------------: |
| Carbohydrates | 224g | 323g |
| Protein | 50g | 174g |
| Fat | 44g | 77g |

That range is intentionally broad. It makes more sense to discuss a protein goal alongside the rest of a person’s diet and activity than to chase one number in isolation.

## Practical vegan protein in Indian food

I have been vegan for three years and have often managed 100g+ of protein a day without protein powder. Soya has been the easiest lever for me: it is a complete protein and fits into a lot of meals I already make.

### Start with soya

- **Soy milk:** I use about 600ml of Sofit soy milk in coffee and tea over the day—roughly 19.2g of protein across two servings.
- **Soya granules or chunks (TVP):** These work in rice, pasta, noodles and sandwiches. About 30g of granules provides roughly 15.6g of protein. For a sandwich, I cook them briefly with water and a sauce or seasoning, then use them as a filling.
- **Tofu:** Tofu is useful in rice, pasta, noodles, sandwiches and scrambles. A 50g serving of the tofu I used then provided about 9.94g of protein.
- **Soya flour:** If roti is a staple, replacing about 30% of wheat flour with soya flour can make a substantial difference. In my example, a 50g roti made with 15g soya flour and 35g wheat increased protein from about 5.45g to 11.61g.

Taken together, 50g tofu in a sandwich, two soya-wheat rotis, 600ml soy milk and 30g soya granules in pasta add up to about 68g of protein before counting the protein in the grains, bread, vegetables and other meals.

### Be smart with ingredients

Even without relying so heavily on soya, I try to make ingredient swaps that make a meal more substantial:

- Use quinoa or barley sometimes instead of rice.
- Add nutritional yeast where it works.
- Use seitan when it suits the meal.
- Build meals around legumes, tofu, soy milk and other protein-containing staples instead of treating protein as an afterthought.

The aim is not to make every meal perfect. It is to make a reasonably protein-aware Indian diet feel ordinary, affordable and repeatable.
